Week 4 was the week that changed the atmosphere of the entire semester. Quizzes started — and for all of us, this was the very first examination experience of university life. Nobody properly knew how to prepare, what format to expect, or how strict the grading would be. The classroom that had felt relaxed and exploratory in the first few weeks suddenly became serious. Friends were discussing topics, sharing notes, and everyone was a little on edge. University ka pehla imtehaan — it felt like a very big deal.

My quiz preparation strategy at that point was, honestly, non-existent. I read the slides a couple of times and hoped for the best. The concept of proper university exam preparation was still completely unknown to me. I did not know how to identify the most important topics, how much depth to go into, or how to practice in a way that actually prepares you rather than just giving you the feeling of having studied. These are skills nobody teaches you directly — you learn them through experience, often through failure.

The Programming Fundamentals quiz, taught by **Dr. Bilal Ahmad**, was where that failure arrived most clearly. In Week 5, the results came back — and I scored **2 out of 10**.

I am not ashamed to write this here because I believe in honesty, and because I think this number will mean something to whoever is reading this blog and going through something similar. Getting 2/10 in your first university quiz is a deeply uncomfortable feeling — especially when you see classmates scoring 7, 8, or even full marks. It was one of the most stressful moments of my first semester. I sat with that paper in my hands and genuinely questioned myself: Am I cut out for this field? Is Computer Engineering the right choice? Will I ever actually understand programming?

But looking back now, that 2/10 was the most important score of my university life — because it forced me to face reality. I could not sit back and passively absorb anymore. The approach I had been taking — showing up to lectures, half-following along, copying lab code without truly understanding it — was clearly not working. That quiz result made it impossible to pretend otherwise.

Dr. Bilal Ahmad, who teaches Programming Fundamentals as part of his broader expertise in AI, ML, and Deep Learning, has a way of making you feel that poor early performance is not a verdict — it is information. He pushes students toward real problem-solving from early on, and the gap between where I was and where that demanded I be became very clear in Week 5. His research focus, which you can explore on [Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com.au/citations?user=8nZ0jVkAAAAJ&hl=en), is on models that work with real-world precision — and that same standard applied to how he expected students to engage with the material.

The other quizzes that week — Discrete Mathematics on set operations, Calculus on integration basics, ICT on computer networks — were not perfect, but they did not carry the same sting. Programming was where I had the biggest gap, and Week 5 made that gap visible in the clearest possible way.

What I did not yet know — what I could not have known sitting there with that paper — was that the second half of the semester would look completely different. That 2/10 would eventually sit in the same story as an 8/10 project presentation, an exhibition appearance, and a final grade of B+. The distance between those numbers is not talent. It is what you decide to do after the low point.

*Failure is simply the opportunity to begin again, this time more intelligently.* — Henry Ford

The question in Week 5 was: what would I actually do next?
