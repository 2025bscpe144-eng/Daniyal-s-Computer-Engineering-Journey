As Weeks 6 and 7 arrived, word spread quickly through the department — midterm exams were approaching. For every single one of us, this was completely uncharted territory. We had never prepared for university-level midterms before. School exams felt nothing like this. The scope was broader, the expectations higher, and the format entirely unfamiliar.

The honest truth about my preparation strategy during those two weeks? I had none. I did not know how much to study, which topics were more important than others, how long university papers actually were, or how strict the marking would be. So I did what most confused students do — I rote-learned whatever I could find in my notes and slides. I crammed. Friends were doing the same. The library became slightly more crowded. WhatsApp groups were full of last-minute notes being shared at odd hours of the night. There was a lot of activity, but not a lot of real understanding being built.

**Programming Fundamentals** was where the gap was most obvious. Dr. Bilal Ahmad's course required genuine conceptual understanding — you could not memorise your way through questions that asked you to trace through code, identify errors, or write functions from scratch. Dr. Bilal Ahmad, who brings his deep expertise in AI and Machine Learning into how he frames even introductory programming concepts, had structured the course so that understanding was the only real path forward. Rote learning might get you through a definitions-based quiz, but it would not carry you through questions that demanded you actually think in code. This is a lesson I was learning the hard way, in real time.

I was preparing for midterms in five subjects simultaneously: Programming Fundamentals, Discrete Mathematics, Calculus I, ICT, and Fehm-e-Quran. Each required a different kind of preparation. DM required understanding logical proofs and set operations deeply — things that could not be paraphrased, only grasped. Calculus was more mechanical and my FSc background helped here. ICT covered fundamentals and networks, which were more conceptual and manageable. Fehm-e-Quran required translation and understanding of Quranic text, which was its own kind of careful study.

One positive thing about Weeks 6 and 7 was that we started talking more to seniors. Their advice was consistent and simple: university papers test understanding, not just memory. They told us that questions are designed to reveal whether you actually know the concept or have just memorised words. We heard that advice and nodded — and then largely continued doing what we were doing, because old habits under pressure are hard to break. The irony is that we understood the advice intellectually but did not yet understand it experientially. That was coming in Week 8.

Throughout all of this, lab manuals continued every single week without exception. Whatever was happening academically — stress, confusion, exam pressure — the labs ran on schedule and the manuals had to be submitted. At the time this felt like an added burden on top of everything else. In hindsight, it was one of the most valuable disciplines of the semester. That consistent weekly output, even when done imperfectly, built a rhythm that the second half of the semester would rely on.

The most important lesson I took from Weeks 6 and 7 was not about any specific subject. It was about preparation culture — and the fact that if you do not build a real study strategy early in the semester, you will be scrambling to compensate for it in the weeks that matter most. The midterms were days away. I was as ready as I was going to be with the approach I had taken. And the results would reflect that honestly.
