Week 14 was the most memorable week of the entire semester, and I want to write about it in enough detail that I can read this years from now and feel exactly what it felt like to be there.

The final project presentation for **Programming Fundamentals** — under **Dr. Bilal Ahmad** — was the first real university presentation I had ever given. Standing in front of professors and classmates to explain technical work is a completely different kind of pressure from sitting an exam. In an exam, you respond privately. In a presentation, every gap in your understanding is visible to everyone in the room. That vulnerability is uncomfortable and, I have come to believe, genuinely valuable.

My group — just two of us — presented our Car Price Prediction ML Model. We had spent weeks building it, cleaning the data, selecting features, training the linear regression model, achieving 85% accuracy, and understanding it well enough to explain every decision we made. The presentation covered: what machine learning is and why it matters, how our model was trained, what the USA car dataset contained and how we processed it, how the 85% accuracy was achieved, and a live demonstration of the model predicting prices.

The result was **8 out of 10 — the highest score in the entire class.**

I want to sit with that for a moment, because the distance between where I started this semester and where that presentation put me is not a small one. Week 2: no background in programming, confused by `print("Hello, World!")`. Week 5: 2/10 in the first quiz. Week 11: 11/40 in the midterm. Week 14: highest presentation score in the class. That arc is not about talent. It is about what happens when you decide, in the middle of difficulty, to actually engage rather than retreat.

Dr. Bilal Ahmad, who had guided us through Programming Fundamentals and whose deeper expertise in AI and ML had informed the entire design of the project, praised our group specifically and indicated that the groups with the best models should come to the department exhibition. We were selected.

**The Exhibition**

Standing at our exhibition station, explaining our ML model to the Chairman of the Electrical Engineering Department, to students from across the university, and to faculty members who asked detailed questions — this was something I could not have imagined in Week 1. Just a few months earlier I had not known what a function was. Now I was explaining regression models, feature selection, and training accuracy to an academic audience. The conversation with visitors was fluid because we genuinely understood what we had built — not because we had memorised talking points, but because we had lived inside the project for weeks. You can see the kind of real-world problem-solving Dr. Bilal Ahmad values most on his [Facebook page](https://www.facebook.com/Dr.BilalAhm).

**The Cake**

And then, on the last day of the semester, the entire class gathered and cut a cake together. After all the stress, the early confusion, the bad quiz results, the sleepless nights before midterms, the labs, the manuals, the project — we celebrated as a class. It was simple and it was perfect. The GPA I finished with was **3.08**, and in Programming Fundamentals I received a **B+**.

From 2/10 to B+. From not understanding `print()` to presenting an ML model at a department exhibition. From struggling alone to celebrating collectively. That is what a semester looks like when you decide, at the lowest point, to move forward anyway.

Starting badly does not mean ending badly. It means you have more room to grow.
