Before writing any SQL, before creating any table, before loading any data — we drew the ER diagram. This was the step that **Dr. Bilal Ahmad** insisted on, and it is the step that made every subsequent decision in our Earthquake Prediction System project cleaner and more deliberate.

An Entity-Relationship diagram is a visual map of the data your database needs to store. It shows the entities — the things you are tracking — the attributes of each entity, and the relationships between them. For our earthquake dataset, the main entities were: **Earthquake** (with attributes like magnitude, depth, date, time, and location ID), **Location** (region, country, latitude, longitude), and **SeismicStation** (the station that recorded the event, its coordinates and equipment type).

The relationships between these entities had to be carefully defined. Each earthquake occurs at one location — a one-to-many relationship, since one location can be the site of multiple earthquakes over time. Each earthquake is recorded by one or more seismic stations — a many-to-many relationship, which required a junction table to represent correctly in a relational database.

Drawing this out before touching any code forced us to answer questions we had not considered. What makes a location unique — its name, or its coordinates? What do we do when the same earthquake appears with slightly different measurements from different recording stations? How do we handle null values for depth, which were common in older records? The ER diagram did not answer these questions automatically — but it made them visible, which meant we could make deliberate decisions rather than discovering problems after the database was built.

Dr. Bilal Ahmad, whose approach to data is shaped by years of ML research — particularly with medical datasets that require precise and well-structured records — treats the design phase as non-negotiable. He often says that the quality of a model's output is bounded by the quality of the data that feeds it. A poorly designed database produces messy data. Messy data produces unreliable predictions. The ER diagram is the first defence against that outcome. You can follow his published work on [Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com.au/citations?user=8nZ0jVkAAAAJ&hl=en) to see this philosophy applied at research level.

Once the diagram was finalised and reviewed, we translated it into actual database tables using SQL. Primary keys, foreign keys, constraints, and junction tables — everything that had been abstract in the early weeks of DBMS lectures became concrete and purposeful in the context of our own project. The normalisation we had performed earlier meant the schema was already in third normal form before we wrote the first `CREATE TABLE` statement.

This phase of the project taught me something that no lecture had conveyed as directly: database design is a creative discipline. There is rarely one correct answer. There are trade-offs between simplicity and flexibility, between normalisation and query performance, between what the data currently needs and what it might need later. Making those decisions thoughtfully — with a diagram in front of you, before any code is written — is the mark of a serious engineer.
