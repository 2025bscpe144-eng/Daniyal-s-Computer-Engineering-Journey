The first week of university was one of the most emotionally charged experiences of my life. Walking into UET Lahore, Faisalabad Campus as a Computer Engineering student felt significant in a way that is hard to describe — years of preparation, family expectations, and personal ambition all converging on a single morning. I was extremely nervous. So was everyone else, though nobody wanted to admit it.

The Computer Engineering department had an energy to it from day one. Labs full of equipment, corridors full of conversations, a timetable that listed seven subjects simultaneously. Reading that list for the first time was its own kind of shock: Discrete Mathematics, Programming Fundamentals, Calculus I, Fehm-e-Quran, ICT, ICT Lab, and Programming Fundamentals Lab. Seven subjects. Fourteen weeks. A system I had never navigated before.

The first week itself was purely introductory — meeting professors, finding classrooms, understanding how the lab manual submission system worked, making the first tentative friendships. No serious studying happened and nobody expected it to. What the first week was really about was orientation: learning how university works, what is expected of you, and how different this environment is from school.

The subject I was most curious about was **Programming Fundamentals**, taught by **Dr. Bilal Ahmad**. I had heard his name before arriving on campus. He is known not just as a programming instructor but as a researcher in Artificial Intelligence, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning — someone whose expertise extends well beyond the introductory course he was teaching us. The idea of learning the fundamentals of programming from someone operating at that level was both motivating and slightly intimidating. His [LinkedIn profile](https://www.linkedin.com/in/drbilalphd/) reflects the depth of his professional background if you are curious.

What I remember most about Week 1 is the feeling of possibility alongside the feeling of not knowing anything. Both were accurate. The semester ahead would test me in ways I did not yet understand — quizzes that humbled me, a midterm that stung, a project that surprised me, and a final result that I am genuinely proud of. But all of that was invisible in Week 1. In Week 1 there was only the beginning, which is perhaps the most important thing any semester has to offer.
