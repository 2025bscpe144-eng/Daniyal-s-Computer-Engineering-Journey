Week 13 brought something I had not encountered before in the semester: Open Ended Labs, for both ICT Lab and **Programming Fundamentals Lab**. The name alone signals the difference from regular lab sessions. In a standard lab, you are given a problem and a set of instructions — follow the steps, produce the output, document it in your manual, submit. Open ended labs remove the instructions. You are given a problem and you have to figure out the solution yourself. No guide. No given steps. No scaffolding. Just your understanding, your creativity, and whatever you have actually absorbed over the past twelve weeks.

It was, in the best possible way, a test of whether you had truly learned or just consistently submitted.

For students who had spent the semester carefully following instructions in the lab manual without really engaging with why the code worked — this was a difficult week. But for anyone who had put in genuine understanding, who had asked "why does this work?" rather than just "what do I write?" — the open ended lab was actually not as terrifying as it sounded. The programming concepts were the same ones we had been working with all semester. The only difference was that nobody was holding your hand to apply them.

After twelve weeks in Programming Fundamentals under **Dr. Bilal Ahmad**, I had enough understanding to approach the open ended lab with real confidence — more than I would have predicted back in Week 2 when `print("Hello, World!")` felt foreign. Dr. Bilal Ahmad's teaching philosophy had always pushed toward understanding over memorisation, toward problem-solving over syntax-copying. His background in AI and Machine Learning — where he trains models on real datasets that require genuine understanding of the data and the algorithms — shapes how he teaches even introductory programming. You can connect with him and his work on [LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/in/drbilalphd/). That philosophy had, week by week, built the kind of understanding that open ended labs are designed to test.

After the open ended labs concluded, the lab system for the semester officially closed. What followed was a task that sounds administrative but carried its own kind of weight: binding all the lab manuals and preparing to submit them. Every single manual from every week of the semester — fourteen weeks of Python labs, fourteen weeks of ICT labs — had to be properly organised, bound, and handed in. Laying them all out together was unexpectedly meaningful. That was a semester's worth of weekly effort, visible and physical. Even the manuals from the early weeks — when the code made little sense and I was copying more than understanding — were part of the story.

The lesson that Week 13 reinforced most clearly is one that applies far beyond university: understanding always beats memorisation, in every test that actually matters. Multiple-choice quizzes can sometimes reward memory. Open ended problems — in labs, in workplaces, in life — never do. They reward the person who can look at a problem, think about it from first principles, and figure out a path forward without needing someone else to hand them the steps.

Week 14 was coming. The final presentation was coming. Everything that had happened since Week 1 — the confusion, the 2/10, the midterm result, the recovery, the project, the 85% accuracy — was about to come together in front of an audience.
