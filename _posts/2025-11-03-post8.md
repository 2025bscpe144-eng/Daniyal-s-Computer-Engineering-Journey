Week 11 was the week the midterm papers came back. I had known the results would not be strong — the experience of sitting in that exam hall and facing questions I was not properly prepared for had made that clear. But there is a difference between knowing abstractly that a result will be difficult and actually holding the paper in your hands and reading the number. For my **Programming Fundamentals** midterm, that number was **11 out of 40**.

Reading that score was genuinely painful. After weeks of attending every lecture, submitting every lab manual, and making what felt like real effort to keep up with the material — 11/40 felt like evidence that the effort had produced nothing. I sat with the paper for a few minutes and felt all the familiar thoughts come back: Am I in the right field? Is this something I can actually do? Why is everyone else seemingly managing better?

But something was different this time compared to the 2/10 quiz result in Week 5. This time I did not spiral. I sat with those feelings for a moment — acknowledged them honestly rather than pushing them aside — and then made a deliberate decision. Isko chor ke aage badhna hai. Leave it. Move forward. That is not giving up; it is strategic recovery. Dwelling on a bad result does not change it. What you do in the weeks after the bad result is what actually defines your semester.

The decision I made in Week 11 had three parts. First: focus completely on the final project and make it genuinely excellent. Second: ensure the remaining lab work was done with real understanding rather than just submission. Third: prepare properly for the final exam rather than repeating the midterm mistake. Those three commitments shaped everything that followed.

My group partner and I used Week 11 to start seriously planning the project. We had been discussing the idea of a Machine Learning model since Week 10, and now we committed to it formally. The plan was to build a **Car Price Prediction System** using Python and ML libraries — a model that could take car features as input (brand, year, mileage, fuel type, engine size) and predict the price with meaningful accuracy. Starting badly does not mean ending badly. It means you have more room to grow.
