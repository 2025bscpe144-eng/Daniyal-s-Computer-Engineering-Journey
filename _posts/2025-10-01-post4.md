I want to write honestly about the hardest parts of my first semester because I think honesty is more useful than a polished version of events that makes it look easier than it was.

The first real blow came in Week 5. Programming quiz results were returned and I had scored **2 out of 10**. I am not ashamed to write that here. Getting 2/10 in your first university subject quiz is a deeply uncomfortable experience — especially when you see classmates scoring 7, 8, or 10. I sat with that paper and genuinely questioned myself: Am I in the right field? Will I ever actually understand programming? Is Computer Engineering the right choice?

Those questions are natural. What matters is what you do with them.

The weeks that followed were a grind. Midterm preparation arrived with no real strategy on my part. I rote-learned whatever I could — rattay lagaye, as we say — without truly understanding the material. The library got slightly more crowded. WhatsApp groups filled with last-minute notes. The collective anxiety of a batch preparing for their first university midterms was palpable everywhere on campus.

The **Programming Fundamentals** midterm, under Dr. Bilal Ahmad, was particularly difficult. His course demanded actual understanding — the questions asked you to think in code, not recite definitions. That is entirely consistent with how Dr. Bilal Ahmad approaches everything, including his AI and ML research, where surface-level familiarity with data is never enough. Real models require real understanding of what the data represents. He holds students to the same standard. When the paper came back, I had scored **11 out of 40**.

Reading that number was genuinely painful. Weeks of attendance, lab submissions, and effort — and 11/40. The feeling of having worked and still fallen short is a particular kind of discouraging. The exam week itself had been brutal: assignments piling up across DLD, Database, and OOP simultaneously, early mornings, barely time to eat, no buffer for the preparation I should have started earlier. The lesson learned the hard way: university does not wait for you to be ready. You prepare early or you pay for it late.

The last weeks of exam season were exhausting in a way that felt avoidable in hindsight — because it was. Better preparation culture, started earlier, would have prevented most of it.

But here is the other side of that story. The 2/10 quiz and the 11/40 midterm were not the end. They were information. They told me exactly where the gaps were and exactly what needed to change. What you do after the low point defines the semester far more than the low point itself. And in my case, what came next was a project — and the project changed everything.

*Starting badly does not mean ending badly. It means you have more room to grow.*
