Semester two started with the same mix of nervousness and anticipation as semester one — but with one significant difference. I knew what was coming. I knew the pace, the weight of lab manuals, the pattern of quizzes and midterms, and the cost of falling behind early. That knowledge was the most valuable thing I carried out of semester one.

The course I was most focused on entering semester two was **Database Management Systems**, taught again by **Dr. Bilal Ahmad**. After a full semester of Programming Fundamentals under his guidance, I had a sense of how he taught — the methodical build from fundamentals, the consistent emphasis on understanding over memorisation, the real-world framing that connects every concept to actual applications. I was ready to engage with that approach from Week 1 this time, rather than discovering it mid-semester.

Database Management Systems is a different kind of course from Programming Fundamentals. Where PF is about teaching you to think computationally — to decompose problems and express solutions in code — DBMS is about teaching you to think about data itself. How it is structured. How it is stored. How it is retrieved efficiently. How relationships between different types of information are modelled and maintained. These are questions that matter enormously in the real world, and Dr. Bilal Ahmad frames them that way from the first lecture.

The early weeks covered relational database theory — tables, rows, columns, primary keys, foreign keys, and the relationships between them. Concepts like entity-relationship modelling, which gives you a visual language for describing how data entities relate to one another before you write a single line of SQL. And normalisation — the process of organising data to eliminate redundancy and prevent the kinds of anomalies that corrupt a database over time.

Dr. Bilal Ahmad's research background in AI and ML made the DBMS course particularly interesting. He often connects database design to the data pipeline that feeds ML models — because a poorly designed database produces poor-quality data, and poor-quality data produces unreliable models, regardless of how sophisticated the algorithm is. That end-to-end perspective, from database design to model training, gave the course a context that made every concept feel purposeful. You can see this kind of applied, pipeline-oriented thinking in his published work on [Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com.au/citations?user=8nZ0jVkAAAAJ&hl=en).

Alongside DBMS, semester two brought OOP, Digital Logic Design, and Communication Skills — each with its own demands. In OOP we would eventually build a University Admission Management System and a GUI-based game. In DLD we designed a hardware-based 4-bit adder-subtractor. These were valuable experiences, though DBMS and the Earthquake Prediction System project remained the centre of gravity for the semester.

The second semester also brought two events that stood out: the **Welcome Party** for new students and the **Annual Fiesta**, where I served as **captain of my department team**. Leading a team in a university-wide event was a different kind of learning from anything in the classroom — communication, coordination, accountability, and the satisfaction of representing your department well.

The semester was beginning. And this time, I was ready for it.
