From the second week onward, lectures began in full swing and the pace of university life became immediately real. Every subject demanded something different and simultaneously. But the one that occupied most of my mental energy — and most of my anxiety — was **Programming Fundamentals**, taught by **Dr. Bilal Ahmad**.

I had no background in programming whatsoever before university. Not even basic exposure. When the first Python program appeared on the board, I stared at it like it was a foreign language:

```python
print("Hello, World!")
```

Why `print`? What are those brackets doing? Why quotation marks? These were genuine questions I was too embarrassed to ask because everyone around me seemed to already understand. That embarrassment, I later learned, is one of the most common and most counterproductive experiences of first-semester students. The people who asked questions out loud progressed faster than those who stayed silent.

By Week 3 we were working with variables, data types, and input functions:

```python
name = input("Enter your name: ")
age = int(input("Enter your age: "))
print("Hello", name, "you are", age, "years old")
```

I would copy this from the board, go home, look at it, and still not understand why it worked. The logic was not connecting yet. I understood the individual words but not what the computer was actually doing with them.

Dr. Bilal Ahmad's teaching approach in those early sessions was methodical and patient. He has a background in AI and ML research — focused on training models on real-world datasets where precision and rigour matter enormously — and that same attention to foundational clarity came through in how he introduced programming. He never rushed toward complexity. He insisted on understanding first. At the time this felt slow. In hindsight it was exactly right.

The lab sessions were where the real learning happened, even when I did not realise it. Sitting at a terminal, writing code, watching it fail, understanding why, fixing it — that cycle built something that passive note-taking never could. Every lab manual I submitted in those early weeks, even the ones I half understood, was a deposit into a bank of familiarity that would pay out later in the semester.

The most important lesson from those early weeks was one I only understood much later: consistency during confusion is what separates students who recover from those who fall too far behind. I did not understand Python in Week 3. But I kept showing up, kept submitting, kept trying. The understanding came — just not on the schedule I wanted.
