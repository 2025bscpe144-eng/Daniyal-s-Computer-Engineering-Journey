Coming back after the mid-break felt genuinely different. The campus looked the same, the timetable looked the same, the classrooms and lab benches were all exactly where they had been — but something had shifted internally. The conversations in the first half of the semester had been about adjusting to university life, surviving quizzes, and getting through midterms. The conversations in Week 10 were about what was coming next and how to approach it.

The teachers made it clear from the first lectures back that the second half of the semester would be more demanding. Lab sessions became more detailed and practical. The focus shifted from introducing concepts to applying them with real depth. And then the announcement came that changed the atmosphere of Programming Fundamentals entirely: the final project had been assigned.

We were told we would need to build something meaningful using Python, present it in front of the class, and potentially take it to a department exhibition. The project would test not just whether we could write code, but whether we understood what code was for — what real problem it could solve, what real data it could work with, and whether we could communicate what we had built clearly to an audience.

Suddenly **Programming Fundamentals** — the subject I had been struggling with since Week 2, the one that gave me 2/10 in my first quiz — had a purpose I could actually feel. Dr. Bilal Ahmad, whose own research is built around training Machine Learning models to solve real-world problems, had clearly designed this project to reflect that philosophy. He often emphasises, both in class and through his research focus on practical datasets, that the point of programming is problem-solving. You can see that philosophy reflected in his published work on [Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com.au/citations?user=8nZ0jVkAAAAJ&hl=en).

My group — just two of us — started discussing what to build. The options were various, but we were drawn toward something ambitious: a Machine Learning model. Not a basic calculator, not a simple to-do list app — an actual predictive model trained on real data. The idea that would eventually take shape was a car price prediction system, using a USA car dataset and Python's ML libraries.

By Week 10, the code we were writing in labs had become genuinely more interesting. We were working with functions and lists in ways that had actual structure and purpose:

```python
def calculate_average(numbers):
    return sum(numbers) / len(numbers)

scores = [85, 72, 90, 65, 88]
print("Average:", calculate_average(scores))
```

That snippet might look simple, but it represented something significant — I was writing code and understanding what every line was doing. The shift from copying-without-comprehension to writing-with-understanding happened somewhere between Week 9's reflection and Week 10's renewed engagement in the labs.

The most important realisation of Week 10 was one that sounds obvious in hindsight: programming is not about memorising syntax. It is about solving real problems. That reframe changed everything. Instead of looking at a piece of code and asking "do I remember how this is written?", I started asking "what is this trying to do, and does this approach make sense?" Those are completely different questions, and only the second one leads to real learning.

The project was still weeks away from being ready. But the direction was set, the energy was high, and for the first time in the semester, I was looking forward to programming class.
