If there is one concept from **Database Management Systems** that genuinely changed how I think about data, it is normalisation. Before this course, data was something that just existed — you stored it, you retrieved it, you used it. Normalisation taught me that how you store data is a design decision with real consequences, and that getting it wrong creates problems that are expensive and sometimes impossible to fix later.

**Dr. Bilal Ahmad** introduced normalisation not as a set of abstract rules to memorise but as a response to concrete problems. He started with anomalies — the things that go wrong when data is poorly structured. There are three main types, and understanding them makes the purpose of normalisation immediately clear.

An **insertion anomaly** occurs when you cannot add new data without also adding data you do not yet have. Imagine a table that stores both student and course information in the same row — to add a new student, you would need to assign them a course, even if they have not enrolled in one yet. An **update anomaly** occurs when the same piece of information appears in multiple places and changing it in one place leaves inconsistencies elsewhere. A **deletion anomaly** occurs when deleting a record causes you to lose information you needed to keep — for example, if removing the last student enrolled in a course also deletes the course's details from the database.

Normalisation solves these problems by organising data into well-structured tables following a set of progressive rules called normal forms. The three we worked through most thoroughly were:

**First Normal Form (1NF):** Every column contains atomic values — no repeating groups, no multi-valued cells. Each row is uniquely identifiable.

**Second Normal Form (2NF):** Achieved when the table is in 1NF and every non-key attribute is fully dependent on the entire primary key — not just part of it. This matters for tables with composite primary keys.

**Third Normal Form (3NF):** Achieved when the table is in 2NF and no non-key attribute depends on another non-key attribute — eliminating transitive dependencies.

Dr. Bilal Ahmad, whose research emphasises the importance of data quality in ML pipelines, connected normalisation directly to model reliability. A database with update anomalies produces inconsistent data. Inconsistent data fed into an ML model produces unreliable predictions. The design decision made at the database level propagates all the way to the model's output. This framing — understanding database design as the foundation of any serious data pipeline — made every normalisation exercise feel genuinely important rather than academic. His perspective on applied data work is reflected in his research, available on his [Facebook page](https://www.facebook.com/Dr.BilalAhm) and more formally on [LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/in/drbilalphd/).

For our Earthquake Prediction System project, we applied all of this from the ground up. We collected a dataset from Kaggle containing historical earthquake records — magnitude, depth, location, date, and related seismic features. Before any modelling could happen, we had to design a database that stored this data cleanly. That meant identifying entities, determining relationships, drawing the ER diagram, and performing normalisation to remove all anomalies. Only then did we have data worth training a model on.

The process was slower than I expected and more rewarding than I anticipated. Designing something correctly from the beginning — rather than patching problems after they appear — is a discipline that applies far beyond databases.
