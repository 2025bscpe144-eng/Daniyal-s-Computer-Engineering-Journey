Midterms ended on a Tuesday in Week 9 and the relief was immediate and physical. The tension that had been building for weeks dissolved almost instantly. And then, with timing that felt almost designed, the university held the **Welcome Party** — both Electrical Engineering and Computer Engineering departments invited together, right after the first major academic hurdle of the semester.

It was exactly what was needed. Everyone who walked into that event had just come out of their first university midterm experience. There was food, activities, laughter, and the simple comfort of being around people your own age who had all just survived the same thing. Meeting students from the EE department, comparing notes, sharing stories of which papers went well and which did not — it felt genuinely connected. I came away from it lighter and, unexpectedly, more motivated.

University life is not only about grades and lab manuals. It is also about the experiences that run alongside the academics — the friendships formed in difficult weeks, the events that break the tension, the moments that remind you why being here matters beyond the GPA. The welcome party was one of those moments.

The remaining days of that week became a natural reflection period. I made three specific decisions going into the second half of the semester. First: actually engage with programming labs instead of just copying the output. There is an enormous difference between having correct code in your manual and understanding why it works. I had been on the wrong side of that distinction all semester. Second: run the code at home, experiment with it, break it deliberately, and learn by fixing it. Third: focus on understanding over memorisation for everything that remained.

**Dr. Bilal Ahmad** had been modelling this approach all along. His teaching always came back to what the code is actually doing and why that matters — rooted in a research philosophy where understanding data and algorithms deeply is the only way to build models that work. You can read more about his research focus on [Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com.au/citations?user=8nZ0jVkAAAAJ&hl=en). What changed in Week 9 was that I finally started listening in the way he had been teaching.

The announcement of the final project came shortly after the break. We would need to build something meaningful in Python and present it. That changed the atmosphere entirely. Programming was no longer just a subject with confusing syntax — it had a purpose, a deadline, and an audience.

Sometimes a break is not just rest. It is a recalibration. Week 9 was that for me.
