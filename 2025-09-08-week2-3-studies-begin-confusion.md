From the second week onward, our proper studies officially began. Lectures started in full swing and suddenly everything felt very fast. Each subject had its own pace and style, and the challenge of keeping up with all seven simultaneously became immediately real. The first week's excitement was still there, but now it was sharing space with confusion, pressure, and the uncomfortable feeling of not understanding something that everyone else seemed to grasp.

The subject that confused me the most — from day one, without any competition — was **Programming Fundamentals**, taught by **Dr. Bilal Ahmad**. I had absolutely no background in programming and honestly had no real interest in it at that point. Everyone around me seemed to be catching on faster and I was just sitting there reading the slides without understanding a single thing. Our very first Python program was the classic Hello World:

```python
print("Hello, World!")
```

Even this simple line felt foreign to me. Why do we write `print`? What are these brackets for? What does the string mean? These were genuine questions in my head — questions I was too embarrassed to ask out loud because it seemed like everyone else already knew the answers. That embarrassment, I later learned, is one of the most common and most damaging experiences of first-semester university life.

Dr. Bilal Ahmad's teaching style in those early weeks was structured and patient. He understood that most of us were coming in with zero programming background. His approach was to build understanding from the ground up — not to rush into complexity, but to make sure the foundations were solid. This is consistent with how he approaches everything, including his ML and AI research, where he insists on understanding data deeply before modelling it. His [LinkedIn profile](https://www.linkedin.com/in/drbilalphd/) gives a sense of the kind of careful, methodical work he does beyond the classroom.

By Week 3, we were working with variables, data types, and basic input/output:

```python
name = input("Enter your name: ")
age = int(input("Enter your age: "))
print("Hello", name, "you are", age, "years old")
```

I would copy this from the board, go home, look at it again, and still not understand why it worked. The logic was not connecting yet. I understood the individual words — name, age, print — but not why they were arranged the way they were, or what the computer was actually doing when it ran those lines.

In other subjects, the picture was slightly better. Discrete Mathematics introduced us to logic and set theory, which had an abstract quality that was challenging but at least felt like something I could study my way into. Calculus I covered derivatives and limits — familiar territory from FSc, which gave me some confidence. ICT was comparatively manageable, covering general computer concepts. And every single week, without exception, we had lab manuals to complete and submit. The lab instructors were strict about deadlines and that discipline — even when I did not fully appreciate it at the time — built a consistency that would matter later.

The adjustment phase of Weeks 2 and 3 was about more than just subjects. It was about adapting to an entirely new system — new timings, new people, new expectations, and a new kind of independence. Nobody reminded you to study. Nobody checked whether you understood. You were expected to figure things out yourself, ask for help when needed, and stay on top of everything regardless of how lost you felt.

Looking back, the most important lesson of those early weeks was one I only understood much later: consistency during confusion is what separates students who recover from those who fall too far behind. I did not understand Python in Week 3. But I kept showing up, kept copying the code, kept submitting the manuals. Understanding, it turned out, would come with repetition — just not immediately.
