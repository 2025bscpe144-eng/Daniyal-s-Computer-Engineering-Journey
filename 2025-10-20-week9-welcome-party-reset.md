By Tuesday of Week 9, the midterm exams were finally over. The relief that followed was physical — you could feel it in the way people walked, talked, and sat in the common areas. The tension that had been building since Week 6 dissolved almost immediately, replaced by a kind of exhausted lightness. We had made it through the first major academic hurdle of university life, imperfect results and all.

And then something happened that made Week 9 genuinely special, entirely apart from academics.

Our university held the **Welcome Party** for all new students — both the Electrical Engineering and Computer Engineering departments were invited together. The timing could not have been more perfect. Every student who walked into that event had just come out of their first university midterm experience, carrying a week's worth of stress, and the party felt like exactly the kind of release that was needed. There was food, activities, laughter, and the simple pleasure of being in a room full of people your own age who had all just survived the same thing. Meeting students from the EE department, hearing their experiences, comparing notes on which subjects were hardest — it felt like a genuinely connected evening.

University life, I was reminded that night, is not only about lectures, lab manuals, quiz results, and GPA. It is about the experiences you collect alongside the grades — the conversations, the friendships, the events, the memories that do not appear in any transcript but shape who you become during these years. The welcome party was one of those moments. I came away from it feeling lighter and, unexpectedly, more motivated.

The remaining days of Week 9 functioned as a natural break — a calm space between the chaos of midterms and the second half of the semester. It gave me time to reflect properly. **Programming Fundamentals**, under Dr. Bilal Ahmad, had been my most difficult subject — and the midterm had confirmed it. But the semester was not over. There were still weeks of labs, a major project on the horizon, and a final exam. The question was whether I would spend those weeks the same way I had spent the first half, or whether I would genuinely do something different.

I made a few decisions during that reflective period that mattered for the rest of the semester. First, I decided to pay real attention in programming labs instead of just copying the code down and moving on. There is a significant difference between having the correct code in your manual and understanding why it works — and I had been on the wrong side of that difference all semester. Second, I decided to actually run the code myself at home, experiment with it, break it deliberately, and figure out how to fix it. That kind of active engagement with programming is the only thing that builds real understanding. Third, I decided to focus on understanding concepts rather than memorising them for the next quiz — to care more about what the material meant than how it might be tested.

Dr. Bilal Ahmad, who connects everything he teaches to real problem-solving — informed by his deep background in AI and Machine Learning — had been modelling this approach all along. The way he explains programming concepts always comes back to what the code is actually doing and why that matters. You can follow his professional work on [LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/in/drbilalphd/). What shifted in Week 9 was that I finally started listening in the way he was teaching, rather than just trying to capture information for the next assessment.

Sometimes a break is not just rest — it is a recalibration. Week 9 was that recalibration. The second half of the semester was about to begin, and I was ready to approach it differently.
