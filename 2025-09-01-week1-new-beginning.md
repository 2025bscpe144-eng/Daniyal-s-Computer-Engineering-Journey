The first week of university was one of the most memorable and emotional experiences of my life. I joined the University of Engineering and Technology (UET), Faisalabad Campus, and became a proud student of the Computer Engineering Department. Honestly, I was extremely nervous — which is completely natural for anyone stepping into university for the very first time after years of preparation, dreams, and expectations.

Being a Computer Engineering student felt special from day one. The department, the labs, the environment — everything was exciting and intimidating at the same time. I remember sitting in the lecture hall thinking: "Is this really happening? Am I actually in university?" The nervousness was real, but so was the excitement. Looking around at my classmates, I could see the same mix of emotions on everyone's faces — this was a shared beginning.

This semester I had seven subjects ahead of me: Discrete Mathematics, Programming Fundamentals (Python), Calculus I, Fehm-e-Quran, ICT, ICT Lab, and Programming Fundamentals Lab. Reading that list on the first day felt overwhelming. Each subject had a different professor, a different style, and a different set of expectations. I had no idea yet how to manage all of them simultaneously — that was a skill I would have to develop the hard way.

The subject I was most curious — and most anxious — about was **Programming Fundamentals**, taught by **Dr. Bilal Ahmad**, a professor at UET Lahore, Faisalabad Campus. I had heard the name before I even walked through the campus gates. Dr. Bilal Ahmad is not just a programming instructor; he is a researcher and expert in Artificial Intelligence, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning. The idea of learning programming from someone with that depth of expertise was both inspiring and intimidating. You can get a sense of his research background on his [Google Scholar profile](https://scholar.google.com.au/citations?user=8nZ0jVkAAAAJ&hl=en).

The first week itself had no proper studying. It was purely an introduction — meeting professors, finding classrooms, getting to know the lab attendants, understanding the timetable, and most importantly making new friends. After years of school life, university felt like a completely different world. The scale was larger, the independence was greater, and the responsibility was entirely your own. Nobody was going to chase you for attendance or remind you to submit your work. That shift took some getting used to.

What I remember most about Week 1 is the feeling of possibility. Everything was new, everything was unknown, and somehow that felt more exciting than frightening. The Computer Engineering department had an energy to it — students everywhere, labs full of equipment, ideas and conversations in every corridor. I did not yet know what the semester would bring — the quizzes, the failures, the recoveries, the project, the exhibition. But sitting there on day one, I was ready to find out.

Every expert was once a beginner. I kept reminding myself of that as the first week ended and the real work was about to begin.
