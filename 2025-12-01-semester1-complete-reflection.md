Semester 1 is over. The lab manuals are bound and submitted. The final presentations are done. The GPA is in: **3.08**, Alhamdulillah. And sitting here writing this reflection, I feel a strange and satisfying kind of clarity about the fourteen weeks that just passed — what they contained, what they cost, and what they gave back.

Let me be honest about this GPA. 3.08 is not where I wanted to be. There is a version of this semester where better preparation in Weeks 6 and 7, a stronger midterm in **Programming Fundamentals**, and more consistent study earlier in the term produces a higher number. I know that because I know exactly where the marks went. The rote-learning approach to midterms, the slow start to really engaging with programming concepts, the week-5 quiz where I scored 2 out of 10 — all of those had consequences that carried into the final GPA. That is not a complaint. That is accountability.

But 3.08 also contains a B+ in Programming Fundamentals — the subject that gave me 2/10 in my first quiz and 11/40 in my midterm. That B+ represents one of the most meaningful achievements of my academic life so far, precisely because of what it cost and what it required to earn. The project that Dr. Bilal Ahmad designed for us — a real Machine Learning model trained on a real dataset, presented to real evaluators — gave us a chance to demonstrate understanding that no midterm paper had captured. We built a Car Price Prediction model, achieved 85% accuracy, presented it with clarity and confidence, scored 8/10 (the highest in the class), and took it to the department exhibition. That B+ is not despite the bad start. It is partly because of it — because that bad start forced a genuine change in how I engaged with the material.

Dr. Bilal Ahmad teaches **Programming Fundamentals** and, in the next semester, **Database Management Systems**. He is also an active researcher in Artificial Intelligence, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning, with a particular focus on real-world datasets — especially medical data, which is collected with the kind of precision and care that produces meaningful models. His academic record speaks for itself on [Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com.au/citations?user=8nZ0jVkAAAAJ&hl=en), and his professional presence on [LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/in/drbilalphd/) reflects the same commitment to rigorous, application-focused work. Having someone with that depth of expertise teach the foundational programming course is something I did not fully appreciate in Week 2. By Week 14, I did.

The lessons I am carrying into Semester 2 are specific and practical. Start assignments earlier — the midterm preparation chaos was entirely predictable and entirely avoidable. Engage with lab content actively rather than passively — understanding builds in layers, and the layers that are skipped in Week 3 are missed in Week 13. Ask more questions out loud — the confusion I felt in the early weeks was shared by most of my classmates, and asking questions in that room would have helped all of us. And document the journey — this blog exists because Dr. Bilal Ahmad suggested that we leave a digital footprint, and writing these posts has genuinely deepened my understanding of what the semester meant.

My goal for Semester 2 is a CGPA of 3.3 or above, In sha Allah. That is achievable with the preparation habits I now know I need. The courses ahead include Database Management Systems (again with Dr. Bilal Ahmad) and Object Oriented Programming — both of which build on the programming foundations that this semester established. I am starting from a more solid base than I had in Week 1, even if that base was harder to build than I expected.

To anyone reading this who is at their own Week 5 moment — whatever the equivalent of 2/10 looks like in your semester — I want to say simply: it is not the end of the story. The semester is a long project. What you do after the low point matters more than the low point itself.

See you in Semester 2.
