Week 8 arrived and with it, the midterm exams. Sitting in a university examination hall for the first time — with an actual university paper placed in front of me — was a feeling I had never experienced before and will not easily forget. The hall was quiet in the way only exam halls are quiet: a dense, concentrated silence full of pencil sounds, paper shuffles, and the occasional nervous cough. It was completely different from school exams, and that difference became clear the moment I looked at the first question.

University exam questions are not designed to test whether you memorised the lecture slides. They test whether you understood them — whether you can apply, explain, extend, and reason with the material rather than just recite it. The questions were longer, required more explanation, and demanded the kind of thinking that rewards genuine understanding and punishes surface-level cramming. I had prepared largely by rote learning. In that exam hall, that showed immediately.

The **Programming Fundamentals** midterm was particularly difficult. Dr. Bilal Ahmad's course had built toward this point over eight weeks, and the exam reflected the full scope of what we had covered — variables, control flow, functions, lists, and problem-solving logic. Questions were not "define a function" — they were "here is a problem, write a function to solve it." That distinction is enormous when your preparation has been about remembering definitions rather than practising code. I wrote what I could, but I left that hall knowing the result would not reflect what I was capable of with better preparation.

The other papers had their own challenges. Discrete Mathematics required working through logical proofs and set operations accurately and completely — there was no partial credit for almost-right reasoning. Calculus I was more familiar from school, so I felt slightly more confident there. ICT covered fundamentals and networks, and the questions were manageable if you had kept up with the lectures. Fehm-e-Quran required careful translation and understanding that benefited from consistent study throughout the semester rather than last-minute revision.

Coming out of midterms, the overwhelming feeling was one of honesty — an uncomfortable but necessary confrontation with the gap between how I had prepared and what the papers actually demanded. This is the moment many students choose one of two responses: give up and coast through the rest of the semester, or reset and attack the second half differently. I did not make that decision immediately. It took the midterm results coming back, the welcome party, and a few days of reflection before the path forward became clear.

Dr. Bilal Ahmad, whose work in AI and ML modelling is grounded in rigorous attention to data quality and model accuracy — you can see his publications on [Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com.au/citations?user=8nZ0jVkAAAAJ&hl=en) — applies that same rigour to how he assesses students. A midterm paper reveals exactly what you know and exactly what you do not. There is no hiding in it. In a strange way, that honesty is the most valuable thing about a difficult exam: it gives you precise information about where the gaps are.

The pain of studying is far less than the pain of not knowing. I understood that sentence as a quote before the midterms. After Week 8, I understood it as a lived experience.
